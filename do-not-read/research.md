It’s based on the international IFRS S2 standard but tailored for Australian law (even though some IFRS features like industry‑specific SASB metrics are optional).

✅ a. Governance

Who is responsible for climate‑related risk oversight (e.g., board and committees).

How responsibilities, competencies and reporting channels are defined.

How climate governance is embedded into strategy and risk oversight.

✅ b. Strategy and Risk Management

Companies must report:

How climate risks are identified, assessed and managed.

Short, medium and long‑term strategic implications of climate change on operations and finances.

Resilience of the strategy under different climate scenarios (including the required scenarios*).

(*Australia’s version retains a legislative requirement to include at least two climate scenarios — one aligned with ~1.5 °C and one where warming exceeds ~2 °C — and discuss strategic implications under those pathways.)

✅ c. Metrics and Targets

Companies must capture and disclose:

Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions:

Scope 1: Direct emissions from owned/controlled sources.

Scope 2: Indirect emissions from purchased energy.

Scope 3: Indirect emissions along the value chain (reported in later years once phased in).

Quantitative targets (e.g., reduction commitments) and progress toward them.

Other climate‑related performance metrics that are material to the business.

📊 Note on Emissions

Emissions must be converted to CO₂‑equivalent using internationally accepted global warming potentials (such as those from the UN’s Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change) if direct measurement is used.

🧑‍💼 3. General Reporting Requirements

The standard also sets out how the disclosures should be captured and presented:

✔ Timing

Climate disclosures must be prepared and published at the same time as the company’s annual financial report, covering the same reporting period.

✔ Location

Disclosures must be clearly identified and included in the annual report or cross‑referenced within the sustainability section, but not hidden in unrelated information.

✔ Consistency & Definitions

Metrics and targets must be clearly defined, consistently labelled, and consistently measured over time.

✔ Judgements & Uncertainties

Companies must disclose key judgements, assumptions and uncertainties used in climate modelling or emissions measurement.

✔ Statement of Compliance

Companies need to explicitly state that their climate disclosures fully comply with AASB S2 requirements.

Emissions measurement systems: For Scopes 1–3 data (internal sensors, energy usage systems, procurement/supply chain data).

List all sites/offices and their emission contributions

Step 2: Data Collection & Sampling

Request site-level data: energy bills, fuel logs, operational records

Collect supporting evidence: invoices, maintenance logs, supplier Scope 3 data

Sample representative periods or high-impact data to validate reporting

Step 3: Validation & Cross-Check

Compare site data vs corporate consolidation

Check calculations of CO₂-e and emission factors

Ensure consistent units, conversion factors, and methodology across sites

Validate Scope 3 estimations using supplier confirmations or standard estimation methods

Step 4: Process & Control Review

Audit whether data collection processes are documented and followed at all sites

Review internal controls: who enters data, who approves changes, who reconciles reports

Verify workflow compliance: site → regional consolidation → corporate system → board approval

Step 5: Reporting & Evidence Storage

Ensure each site maintains an audit-ready record (digital or physical)

Check that central system aggregates all site data correctly

Document assumptions, calculation methods, and gaps

ESG / Sustainability team feedback

Data accuracy and reliability

Site-level compliance issues

Gaps or weaknesses in methodology

Recommendations for process improvement

High-risk sites first: prioritize sites contributing most emissions
